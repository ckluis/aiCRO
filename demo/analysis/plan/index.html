The Plan · Marigold Home Cleaning · 13 June 2026

Six gates and phases, in order, sized to your real week

How to use this document

This is the companion to the book — the same 90-day argument, turned into tasks you can tick off. It runs in order: two gates first (get the keys, then fix the five things that quietly cost you trust), then the four build phases. The first day costs about 95 minutes of Renata's time and unblocks everything else.

The gates are gates. A phase below a gate does not start until the gate above it is green — out-of-order work wastes the work that follows it. Every task names an owner, an estimate in minutes, the day to do it, and exactly where to do it. Tasks that have a "why" link back to the chapter that argues for them.

If you only do one thing today: finish Phase −1 (≈45 min) so the developer can start the booking flow tomorrow. Nothing converts until someone can actually edit the site and take a card.

Access gate

Phase 1 — Booking + instant price

Weeks 1–3 · fixes D1, D2, D5

The one phase that is non-negotiably first. It is done when a buyer who has never spoken to Marigold can see a real price and confirm a clean without a phone call. Until then, every other improvement just qualifies demand the site can't capture.

Where: CMS → Pages → New page "Book" · CMS → Components → Quote card → set default frequency = Every 2 weeks · CMS → Navigation → Primary button → label + link to /book
Why the price goes on the hero, not behind a form

"Call for a quote" reads as "we'll size you up." The busy professional who would happily pay $149 to never think about it again reads opacity as a red flag. A real number on screen turns hesitation into a booking before the impulse cools.

The number that proves it: share of sessions that reach a priced quote; first self-serve bookings with no callback. · Why → Ch. 1 · D1, the buyer cannot buy · Ch. 4 · What the rebuilt site does

Credibility gate

Phase 2 — Surface the trust

Weeks 3–6 · fixes D3, D6

Once a buyer can book, this decides whether they do. The point is mechanical, not decorative: with trust sitting at the moment of commitment, the flow stops competing on price — the one ground where a $10-cheaper app wins. Starts only after the Phase 1 access gate is green.

Where: CMS → Components → Trust-bar → enable sitewide under hero · CMS → Book → insert "Why you can book with confidence" before payment step · CMS → Pages → New page "Our team"
Why the trust story moves into the path

Marigold pays real money to run a W-2 payroll and background-check every cleaner — exactly what a nervous "who am I letting into my house" buyer wants — and the old site mentioned it in a footer line nobody reads. The most expensive, most defensible asset was the least visible.

The number that proves it: quote→book conversion rate lift, now that trust sits in the path. · Why → Ch. 2 · The honest moat · Ch. 1 · D3

Aim gate

Phase 3 — Emphasize recurring

Weeks 6–9 · fixes D4

With a trusted flow working, point it at the product that compounds. A recurring household is worth roughly eleven one-time cleans of value per year — so the share of new bookings that are recurring is the number that actually grows the business. Make recurring the obvious default and the one-time clean the exception.

Where: CMS → Pages → New page "Recurring" · CMS → Components → Quote card → default frequency + helper line · CMS → Reviews → sort/pin recurring testimonials first
Why aim, not just build

The old site treated a one-time clean and a recurring plan as equals on a flat list. They are not equals — burying the recurring plan in an undifferentiated grid trains demand toward the lowest-value, highest-churn product. Make the high-LTV plan the path of least resistance.

The number that proves it: share of new bookings that are recurring vs. one-time. · Why → Ch. 1 · D4 · Ch. 3 · Group 1, recurring households

Demand gate

Phase 4 — Demand, paced to the team

Weeks 9–13 · funds the recurring base & channels

Only now — with a flow that takes a buyer, reassures them, and points them at recurring — is it safe to pour demand in. And it is poured in metered, because the team of roughly 14 W-2 cleaners is the moat and also a ceiling: a new recurring household handed a rushed or rotating clean is the one failure the brand cannot survive.

The capacity trap to refuse

Do not buy demand the team cannot serve to the standard the site promises. A recurring household won and then handed a rushed clean churns fast, leaves the review that contradicts the whole position, and costs more to lose than it cost to win. If demand outruns capacity, the answer is a short waitlist or a hire — never a lowered standard. One-time deep cleans receive no proactive spend; the door stays open, the dollar goes to the recurring base.

The number that proves it: cost per acquired recurring client; realtor and STR accounts opened. · Why → Ch. 4 · The capacity-honest first moves · Ch. 3 · Groups 2 & 3
