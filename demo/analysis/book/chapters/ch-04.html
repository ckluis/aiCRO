Three things are worth pointing out before you click, because they are the spine of the rebuild and easy to walk past as cosmetics.

Why the price is on the hero, not behind a form

The single most important pixel on the site is the every-2-weeks price the buyer sees without asking for it. It removes the "call for a quote" friction that Chapter 1 named as a critical leak, and it pre-frames the high-value product as the default. A buyer who sees $149/visit before they commit anything reads transparency as confidence; a buyer asked to "size up" with a form reads it as a trap. The card does the work the old contact form blocked.

Second, the trust story now lives in the booking path, not on an About page nobody reaches. The trust-bar — insured & bonded, W-2 team, background- checked, 24-hour re-clean guarantee — sits under the hero on every page, and the Book page repeats it as a "why you can book with confidence" section directly above the card field. This is deliberate: the buyer's hesitation peaks at the moment of commitment, so the reassurance has to be there, not three clicks away. Third, the stale "$25 off" popup is gone — no on-load interrupt, no dead SPARKLE25 code manufacturing distrust at the card field. A premium, trust-led brand does not open with a discount it can't honor.

The two gates the plan is built around

The 90-day plan is not a list of improvements done in order of how nice they'd be. It is sequenced behind two gates, and a phase only opens once the gate before it is passed — because a fix delivered out of order quietly wastes the fix that follows it. Get the gates right and the order of the four phases is no longer a matter of taste; it is forced.

The first is the access gate: can a buyer with a card in hand actually complete a booking, today, without waiting for a callback. Until that is true, nothing else matters — a more trustworthy site that still can't take a booking just loses better-qualified demand. The second is the credibility gate: does the trust story sit inside the path the buyer takes to that booking, so the moat does the closing. Exhibit · shows how the two gates order the work.

The logic of Exhibit · is the whole argument for the order. Spending on demand (Phase 4) before the access gate is open is the most expensive mistake available — it pays to drive buyers to a site that asks them to call and wait. Surfacing trust (Phase 2) before booking works (Phase 1) reassures a buyer who then can't act on the reassurance. So the plumbing comes first, then the trust that sits inside it, then the aim at recurring, and only then the demand — each phase passing the gate that makes the next one worth doing.

The four phases, in order

Each phase is roughly three weeks, owner-runnable with a developer for the booking build, and measured by one number that tells you the gate has actually passed — not "the page shipped" but "the page changed the behavior it was meant to." Exhibit · is the four phases on one screen; the full plan, with task-level owners, minutes, and CMS click-paths, is the companion document.

Phase 1 — Booking + instant price. This is the access gate, and it is the only phase that is non-negotiably first. Ship the 60-second flow with a real price on screen and the flat pricing tables, and make the primary call to action everywhere "See your price & book." Killing the stale promo popup belongs in this same pass, not as a separate task — it actively harms the flow it sits in front of. The phase is done when a buyer who has never spoken to Marigold can reach a price and confirm a clean without a phone call.

Phase 2 — Surface the trust. Once a buyer can book, the credibility gate decides whether they do. Move the trust-bar under the hero, put a "why you can book with confidence" block inside the booking path, tell the Renata Cole hotel-grade story on the team page, and land the "trusted with a key" headline sitewide. The point is mechanical, not decorative: with trust sitting at the moment of commitment, the flow stops competing on price — the one ground where a $10-cheaper app wins.

Phase 3 — Emphasize recurring. With a trusted flow working, the aim gate points it at the product that compounds. The dedicated recurring page, the price card defaulting to every-2-weeks, the "most Durham homes choose every 2 weeks" framing, and recurring reviews surfaced first all make the standing plan the obvious default and the one-time clean the exception. The number to watch is the share of new bookings that are recurring — because at roughly eleven one-time cleans of value per year, that share is what actually grows the business.

Phase 4 — Demand generation. Only now, with a flow that takes a buyer, reassures them, and points them at recurring, is it safe to pour demand in. Local SEO for "recurring house cleaning Durham," a referral mechanic for the 94%- retention recurring base, and paced outreach to realtors and short-term-rental hosts open the two channels that feed the primary group. One-time deep cleans receive no spend — the door stays open, the dollar goes elsewhere.

Why demand comes last, not first

The instinct under pressure is to buy traffic now. Resist it. Every dollar of demand spent before Phase 1 closes pays to send buyers to a site that asks them to call and wait — it funds the leak. The plan deliberately holds demand until the funnel converts, so that the first ad dollar arrives at a flow that captures it rather than spilling it. Demand is the reward for a finished machine, not the way you start building one.

The capacity-honest first moves

There is one thing this plan refuses to pretend, and it changes how Phase 4 is run. Marigold is a real company with roughly 14 W-2 cleaners — confirm, matched into small named teams. That is the moat — and it is also a ceiling. Unlike a gig marketplace that conjures supply on demand, Marigold cannot absorb a flood of new recurring households overnight without breaking the very same-team consistency it sells. A plan that floored the gas on demand would, if it worked, manufacture the one failure the brand cannot survive: a new client matched to a rushed, rotating, or apologetic team.

So the first moves are paced to the team, not to the funnel. The honest sequence is to fix conversion (Phases 1–3) on the existing demand first — that lifts revenue per visitor with zero added load on the cleaners — and to open Phase 4 demand in a metered way, sized to the recurring slots the team can actually staff well. Exhibit · lays out what capacity-honest looks like in practice.

The capacity trap to refuse

Do not buy demand the team cannot serve to the standard the site promises. A new recurring household acquired and then handed a rushed or rotating clean churns fast, leaves the review that contradicts the whole position, and costs more to lose than it cost to win. If demand outruns capacity, the right move is a short waitlist or a hiring plan — never a quietly lowered standard. The moat is the W-2 team showing up the same way every time; spending in a way that breaks that is spending against yourself.
