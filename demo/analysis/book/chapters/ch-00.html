What this chapter establishes

Marigold's site is built to describe a cleaning service when its buyer is trying to book one. The single number that captures the gap: of every visitor ready to buy, none can get a price or a booking without waiting for a callback — the one step the gig apps removed years ago. Everything else in this book follows from closing that gap and aiming the fixed flow at the one product that compounds: the recurring household plan.

The decision this supports: approve a 90-day rebuild that puts a 60-second self-serve booking flow with a real price on screen, then bends the whole site toward recurring. The first move costs you 20 minutes today.

The one-number diagnosis

Strip away the cosmetics and the live marigoldhome.co site has one fault that dwarfs the rest: a buyer with a card in hand cannot complete the purchase. Every path — the hero, the services grid, the pricing section — ends at the same place: call us, or fill a contact form and wait 24–48h callback — confirm. In a category where people decide at 11pm after a long day, that wait is where the sale dies. The buyer books whoever answers first, which is almost always a gig app.

So the diagnosis is one number, and the number is zero: zero ways to see a price and book online, today, on a site whose entire competitive advantage is being a real company the apps can't be. The trust assets Marigold actually pays for — a W-2 team, background checks, the 24-hour re-clean guarantee — are real and rare, and they are buried where the booking-ready visitor never reaches them.

The positioning thesis, in one line

The home cleaning you'd trust with a key — a vetted, W-2 team that shows up the same way every time, booked in 60 seconds online. The position carried sitewide. The buyer's real anxiety isn't "will it be clean" — it's "who am I letting into my home, and will it be the same person next time."

This is not a slogan; it is a moat stated out loud. The category is run by marketplaces that dispatch a different 1099 contractor each visit. Three things Marigold already does are expensive enough that the gig model structurally cannot copy them — a W-2 team it trains and stands behind, the same small named team returning to learn your home, and a 24-hour re-clean guarantee a real company can honor. The whole site's job is to make those three facts impossible to miss, and to put them inside the booking path rather than on an About page.

The 20-minute first action

You do not need the rebuild to start. The cheapest, highest-trust win is also the fastest: turn off the stale "$25 off" popup today. It fires on load with a code (SPARKLE25) that expired last season and now errors at checkout — so it interrupts your readiest buyers and manufactures distrust at the worst possible moment. A premium, trust-led brand does not lead with a discount it can't honor.
