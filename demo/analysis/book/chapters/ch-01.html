1 Part I · Diagnosis — Chapter 1

Where you are

What this chapter establishes

The live marigoldhome.co site is well-built brochure-ware, and that is the problem: it is engineered to describe a cleaning service to someone who is trying to book one. Read as a defect log, it has six named leaks — two of them critical — and they share a single shape. Marigold spends real money on the things that win this category (a W-2 team, background checks, a 24-hour guarantee) and then hides them, while asking the readiest buyer to stop, call, and wait.

The decision this supports: agree on the six leaks and their order, so the rebuild fixes the conversion plumbing first (D1, D2) and only then aims it at trust (D3) and the recurring plan (D4). Severity is rated by revenue impact, not cosmetics.

How to read a defect log

What follows is the current site written up the way an engineer writes up a broken build: each defect gets an ID, a severity, the evidence that it is real, and the fix. There is nothing here about colors or fonts — the site looks fine. The faults are all in what the site does to demand: where a person who arrived ready to hand you money is sent instead to a dead end. A severity of Critical means the defect is losing booking-ready visitors today; High means it is leaving Marigold's structural advantage on the table; Medium means it actively erodes trust or compounds the rest.

Exhibit · is the whole log on one screen — the six defects, sorted by severity, each with its fix. The sections after it walk the two critical leaks and the two high ones in the detail they earn. Read the exhibit first; the prose only adds the evidence.

D1 · The buyer cannot buy Critical

This is the defect that dwarfs the others, and it is the simplest to state: a visitor with a card in hand cannot complete the purchase. Trace any path on the live site and it ends in the same place. The hero button reads "See your price & book" — and clicking it does not show a price; it opens a contact form. The services grid, the pricing band, the closing call-to-action all funnel to the same form or a phone number. The buyer is asked to describe their home, submit, and then wait for a callback.

Where the sale dies

The buyer in this category decides at 11pm, after a long day, when the house is finally quiet. A 24–48h callback — confirm means you reach them on Tuesday afternoon, mid-meeting, after the impulse has cooled and someone else has already started Friday. The gig apps removed the wait years ago. That single removed step is why a high-intent visitor books them instead of you.

Notice the cruel detail: the site already shows a mock instant-price card in the hero — home size, frequency, a $149 every-2-weeks figure, a "Book this clean" button. It looks like the flow exists. It does not function; it is a static picture that, when clicked, drops the buyer into the same callback form. The site promises 60-second booking in its own copy (measured — the words "Book your first clean online in 60 seconds" are on the home page) and then cannot deliver it. That gap between promise and behavior is itself a trust cost.

The fix. A real 60-second self-serve booking flow with a live price on screen — realized in the rebuild as a dedicated booking page plus a working quote card carried on every page. No callback anywhere in the path. This is Phase 1 of the plan, and everything else in this book supports it.

D2 · "Call for a quote" pre-selects the wrong buyer Critical

D1 stops the purchase; D2 poisons who shows up to attempt it. Hidden pricing does not read as "premium" — it reads as "we'll size you up and charge what we think you'll pay." That sentence repels the exact buyer Marigold most wants: the busy professional who would happily pay $149 to never think about cleaning again, and who treats opacity as a red flag. What it attracts instead is the price-anxious bargain-hunter who calls three services to compare quotes — the lowest-value, highest-churn customer in the category.

Why transparency is the premium move here

The reflex is to hide price so you can negotiate up. In a trust-led category the opposite is true: a published flat price says "the price you see is the price you pay" — which is the same promise as the same-team, same-standard moat, applied to the invoice. Opacity quietly contradicts the whole brand. Naming the number is how a real company behaves; making the buyer ask is how a gig app behaves.

The fix. Publish flat per-visit pricing for the common 2–3 bedroom home — $129 weekly / $149 every-2-weeks / $189 monthly — confirm against your books — with larger homes and first-time deep cleans resolving to an exact number inside the flow before any commitment. The good news: this work is already partly done in the rebuilt pricing band; the live site simply doesn't connect it to a booking.

D3 · The most expensive asset is the least visible High

Here is the defect that should sting most, because Marigold has already paid for the fix and then thrown it away. Running a W-2 payroll instead of dispatching 1099 contractors costs real money. Background-checking every cleaner costs real money. Carrying insurance and standing behind a 24-hour re-clean guarantee costs real money. These are exactly the facts a nervous "who am I letting into my house" buyer is desperate to hear — and on the live site they appear once, in a footer line, where no one mid-decision will ever scroll.

The principle the rebuild enforces: trust has to sit in the booking path, not on an About page. A reassurance the buyer reads after they've already decided is a reassurance that arrived too late. So the trust assets move to a persistent trust-bar directly under the hero — insured & bonded · W-2 team · background-checked · 24-hour re-clean guarantee — and get a dedicated "why you can book with confidence" section on the conversion page itself, where the card field is.

D4 · The site sells the cheap product the hardest High

The live services grid lists six things Marigold cleans, all the same size, all the same weight: recurring plans next to a one-time deep clean next to a move-out, as if they were equals. They are not equals. A recurring household is worth roughly twelve one-time cleans every year it stays — and at 94% staying past six months — confirm, it stays. Presenting them as peers trains demand toward the lowest-value, highest-churn product the business offers.

Every-2-weeks plan = $149/visit × 26 visits/yr ≈ $3,874/yr vs. one-time deep clean $329 → ≈ 11.8×

$3,874 ÷ $329 ≈ 11.8 one-time cleans of value per year pricing & cadence — confirm. Over two years a biweekly household clears roughly $7,700. Chapter 3 follows this arithmetic to its conclusion — fund recurring, defund the one-time chase — but the site-level consequence is already clear: recurring should be the spine, not one card in a grid.

The fix. A dedicated recurring landing page; the instant-price card defaulting to every-2-weeks rather than one-time; and the honest framing the reviews already support — "most Durham homes choose every 2 weeks." Make recurring the obvious default and the one-time clean the exception.

D5 and D6 · The avoidable wounds Medium

The last two defects are lower severity but worth naming because one of them is the cheapest fix in the entire engagement. D5 is a "$25 off your first clean" modal that fires on page load with a code (SPARKLE25) that expired last season and now errors at checkout. It interrupts the readiest buyers, and then — at the worst possible moment, right before the card field — the dead code manufactures distrust. A premium, trust-led brand does not lead with a discount it can't honor. Killing the popup removes harm rather than adding work, needs no developer, and is the recommended 20-minute first action from Chapter 0.

D6 is the generic "we clean homes" headline that claims no ground. On its own it is cosmetic; in context it is compounding — with no distinct promise, transparent pricing reads as a commodity price rather than a premium one, so D6 quietly makes D2 worse. The fix is the entire next chapter: stake the position only Marigold can take.

The takeaway, and what it sets up

Sort the log by severity and it reads as a work order. D1 and D2 are losing booking-ready demand this week — they are conversion plumbing, and plumbing comes first. D3 and D4 are leaving Marigold's structural advantage and its best revenue on the table — they are about aiming the fixed flow once it works. D5 and D6 are avoidable wounds — one fixed in 20 minutes, one answered by the positioning chapter. Fix the mechanics, then aim them.

The one good problem to have

Every defect here is one Marigold is uniquely positioned to fix, because the underlying assets already exist and are real. The W-2 team, the background checks, the guarantee, the recurring retention — they are all built and paid for. The site simply fails to show them and fails to let anyone book. That is the best kind of diagnosis: nothing to manufacture, only to surface. Chapter 2 names the position that makes those assets do the selling.
