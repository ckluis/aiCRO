3 Part III · Customers — Chapter 3

Customers worth choosing

What this chapter establishes

Marigold serves five kinds of buyer, and they are not worth the same. Once you put a dollar figure on each — what one of them is worth over the time they stay — four of the five collapse into a clear order and the fifth quietly removes itself from the spend. The arithmetic is blunt: a recurring household is worth roughly twelve one-time cleans every year it stays, and at high retention it stays for years. That single ratio decides where the money and the whole rebuilt site point.

The decision this supports: name recurring busy households as the one group the site is built to win, treat realtors and short-term-rental hosts as the two repeatable channels that feed it, keep post-renovation work as an on-ramp — and stop spending a dollar to chase the one-time deep clean. Every figure below re-multiplies; check the arithmetic.

Why choosing is the point

A service that says yes to everyone aims its site at no one. The home page headline, the default in the price card, the photos, the order of the menu — each is a small bet on who walks in. Spread those bets evenly across five buyers and the site speaks to an average buyer who does not exist. So the work of this chapter is not to rank customers by who is nicest to serve; it is to rank them by what one of them is worth over the life of the relationship, and then to let that number, not sentiment, decide where the site and the spend point.

The unit we will use throughout is the value of a single acquired customer over the time they stay — what a marketer would call lifetime value. It is the honest way to compare a buyer who pays once with a buyer who pays every two weeks for years, and it is the lens that turns a flat list of five services into a clear order of four-plus-one. Exhibit · is that order on one screen; the sections after it walk each group and show the arithmetic.

Read Exhibit · down the "worth" column and the strategy is already visible. The recurring household and the two business channels that feed it are worth thousands a year each; the one-time clean is worth three hundred dollars and then nothing. The rest of the chapter puts each group on its own line, with an envelope — who they are in one breath — and a worked-out figure that re-multiplies so you can check the number rather than trust it.

Group 1 · Recurring busy households Primary

Envelope. Dual-income professional households in and around Durham — Trinity Park, Hope Valley, Watts-Hillandale — who will gladly pay to never think about cleaning again, and who want one reliable team rather than a different stranger every visit. This is the buyer the whole positioning was built for in Chapter 2, and the buyer every other group ultimately feeds.

The reason this group is the spine of the business is not that there are more of them. It is that one of them, once acquired, pays for years. The arithmetic is the argument:

Every-2-weeks plan = $149/visit × 26 visits/yr ≈ $3,874/yr
Weekly plan = $129/visit × 52 visits/yr ≈ $6,708/yr

And it does not stop at one year. With 94% of recurring households staying past six months — confirm, a biweekly home clears roughly $3,874 × 2 ≈ $7,748 — call it $7,700 over two years. Set that beside a one-time deep clean at $329 and the same acquired customer is worth $3,874 ÷ $329 ≈ 11.8 one-time cleans every year they stay — about 23 over two years. This is where the spend goes.

Why the primary call is this group, not the biggest invoice

A short-term-rental property is worth more per year on paper (next section), but it is a narrow channel of a few dozen hosts. The recurring household is both high-value and the largest, most reachable pool in the market — and it is the buyer the "trusted with a key" position uniquely wins. High value times broad reach times a position only you can take is the definition of a primary group. The site's home page, its default price card, and its dedicated recurring landing page all exist to convert this one buyer.

Group 2 · Realtors — the channel that refers Channel

Envelope. Agents who need a documented, on-the-day deep clean that makes a listing show-ready or returns a seller's deposit — and who, once they trust a vendor, reuse the same one across deal after deal. You acquire the agent once; the agent brings the jobs.

Listing / move-out clean $329 × ~2 jobs/mo (24/yr) ≈ $7,896/yr ⇒ vs. biweekly household $3,874 ≈ ~2×

$329 × 24 ≈ $7,896, with no acquisition cost after the first job, so one loyal agent is worth roughly two recurring biweekly households ($7,896 ÷ $3,874 ≈ 2.0) deal volume — confirm per agent. The second, larger payoff is indirect: a realtor stands at the exact moment a buyer is setting up a new home — the peak "we need a cleaner" moment — and can refer that household straight into Group 1.

That dual return is why realtors are a channel, not just a customer: the direct revenue is good, the referral flow into the primary group is better, and both come from acquiring one relationship rather than one job.

Group 3 · Short-term-rental hosts — recurring in disguise Channel

Envelope. Airbnb and short-let hosts who need a reliable same-day turnover — linen reset, a photo checklist, timed to checkout — so a missed clean never costs them a review. Their whole business depends on the turnover happening, which makes them both high-frequency and extremely reluctant to switch.

Turnover $119 × ~6/mo (72/yr) ≈ $8,568/yr → 2-property host ≈ $17,136/yr

$119 × 72 ≈ $8,568 per property; a two-property host is $8,568 × 2 ≈ $17,136/yr turnover frequency & price — confirm. The switching risk is the moat here: a host who changes cleaners gambles their ratings, so retention is structurally high. Functionally this is a recurring plan wearing a different label — the same compounding value as Group 1, sold to a business buyer.

Group 4 · Post-renovation cleanup — the on-ramp On-ramp

Envelope. Homeowners and contractors who need construction dust, paint flecks, and film cleared so a finished project finally looks finished. It is project-driven, not recurring — but it puts Marigold in a freshly renovated home at the precise moment the owner is most likely to want it kept that way.

Reno clean $449 + ~18% convert × $3,874 recurring = $449 + $697 ≈ $1,146 blended

The job itself is a larger-scope deep clean at $449. Its real value is the on-ramp: roughly 15–20% convert to a standing plan — confirm once they see a finished-clean home, so each job is worth $449 now plus an expected 0.18 × $3,874 ≈ $697 of recurring — about $1,146 blended. Worth accepting and serving well; not worth a dedicated acquisition push, because the dollar still converts harder aimed straight at Group 1.

Group 5 · One-time deep cleans Defunded

Envelope. People who want a single seasonal or pre-event deep clean and have no intention of returning. Useful cash, lowest value, highest churn — and, on the old site, the product the menu sold the hardest.

One-time deep clean = $329, once → ~$0 LTV after vs. 2-yr biweekly $7,748 ≈ ~4%

$329 once, with no second visit assumed, is worth roughly 4% of a two-year biweekly household ($329 ÷ $7,748 ≈ 4.2%) conversion negligible — confirm. The booking path stays open — it pays for itself and seeds a few conversions into Group 1 — but it earns zero proactive acquisition spend.

The explicit not-pursuing line

Marigold will not spend a marketing dollar chasing the one-time deep clean. Keep the door open for the buyer who finds it; aim no ad, no SEO page, no outreach, and no home-page emphasis at it. Every dollar spent acquiring a one-time customer is a dollar not spent acquiring a recurring household worth nearly twelve of them a year — so the choice makes itself. Defunding is not neglect; it is the discipline that funds everything above it.

The takeaway, and what it sets up

Put the five groups in one sentence: fund recurring households, feed them with realtors and short-term-rental hosts, accept post-renovation as an on-ramp, and defund the one-time chase. The arithmetic forced that order — an acquired recurring household is worth about 11.8 one-time cleans a year, the channels each refer or repeat into that same pool, and the one-time clean returns roughly 4% of a two-year recurring relationship. Nothing here is a guess about who is pleasant to serve; it is a ranking by what a customer is worth. Chapter 4 takes this ranking and the position from Chapter 2 and builds the site that aims at them — the rebuilt pages and the 90-day plan that ships them.
