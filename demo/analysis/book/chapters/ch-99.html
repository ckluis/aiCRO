Recurring plan
A household that books Marigold on a standing schedule — weekly, every two weeks, or monthly — instead of a single one-off clean.
The product the whole rebuild is bent toward. A recurring household keeps the same small team, a flat per-visit price, and no contract to walk away from. It is worth far more than a one-time clean over the time it stays — a biweekly home clears roughly $7,700 over two years — confirm versus $329 for a single deep clean — which is why the instant-price card defaults to every-two-weeks rather than one-time. "Most Durham homes choose every two weeks" is the framing that makes recurring the obvious default and one-time the exception.
Lifetime value
The total money one customer brings in over the whole time they stay with Marigold — not just their first booking.
The reason a recurring household and a one-time clean are not equals even though both start with one booking. A one-time deep clean is $329, once; a biweekly household is ~$3,874 a year and ~$7,700 over two years — confirm, because 94% stay past six months — confirm. That gap — one recurring client worth roughly 11.8 one-time cleans a year — is the single arithmetic fact that decides where Marigold spends to grow. Internally we call it LTV; in anything a customer reads we say "what they're worth over the time they stay."
Gig apps
Marketplace apps that dispatch a different freelance cleaner to your home each visit — a rotating stranger, booked through a phone screen.
The competitors Marigold is positioned against, not beside. Their whole model is interchangeable, rotating 1099 labour, which is exactly what makes them fast to book and impossible to trust with a key. They removed the callback-and-wait step from booking years ago — which is why a Marigold visitor who can't get an instant price books whoever answers first, usually a gig app. Marigold beats them on the three things they structurally can't copy (see moat); it must not try to beat them on price or speed-of-signup alone.
W-2 team
Cleaners Marigold employs directly — on payroll, trained, equipped, and insured — instead of freelancers hired job-by-job.
"W-2" is the U.S. tax form for an employee; "1099" is the form for a freelancer. The distinction matters here because it is expensive: a W-2 payroll, with its training, equipment, insurance, and background checks, is a cost a gig marketplace optimising for cleaner supply structurally cannot carry. That cost is the point — it's why Marigold's roughly 14 W-2 cleaners are a real, verifiable trust asset and a rotating freelancer is not. On the old site this fact lived in a footer line nobody read; the rebuild promotes it into the trust-bar and the booking path.
Same-team consistency
Each home is matched to one small, named Marigold team that returns every visit and learns the place — not a new face each time.
The second leg of the moat. The buyer's real anxiety in this category is not "will it be clean" but "who am I letting in, and will it be the same person next time." A standing team learns where things go, the dog's name, the squeaky door — and a gig marketplace whose premise is interchangeable labour cannot offer it without contradicting itself. It is the operational proof behind the "trusted with a key" headline, and it is most valuable to recurring households, who feel it visit after visit.
Moat
The handful of things Marigold does that a competitor can't easily copy — the durable reasons to choose it over a cheaper app.
Marigold's moat is three honest, expensive facts: a W-2 team, same-team consistency, and a 24-hour re-clean guarantee. "Honest" is the operative word — each one costs real money and is verifiable, which is precisely why it's hard to copy and easy to believe. A moat made of claims anyone could make is no moat; a moat made of payroll and accountability is. The entire site's job is to make these three impossible to miss, so the moat does the selling that a lower price otherwise would.
Positioning
The one clear thing Marigold stands for in the buyer's head — the ground it claims that no rival can claim back.
Marigold's position is "the home cleaning you'd trust with a key": a vetted, W-2 team that shows up the same way every time, booked in 60 seconds online. The old headline — "we clean homes" — claimed no ground, so the buyer had no reason to pick Marigold over the app that's $10 cheaper. Generic positioning makes every other defect worse, because without a distinct promise transparent pricing just reads as a commodity price. Good positioning is carried in every headline; it is not an About-page paragraph. (Where you see "position" linking here, it's this idea in verb form — staking that ground.)
Severity
How much money a problem is leaking — not how ugly it looks. The ranking that decides what gets fixed first.
Every defect in the diagnosis is rated by revenue impact, borrowed straight from a bug tracker: CRITICAL problems are losing booking-ready demand today (no online booking, no instant price); HIGH problems leave Marigold's structural advantage and its best revenue on the table (the trust story buried, recurring not the emphasis); MEDIUM problems compound the others (a stale popup, generic positioning). Severity, not cosmetics, sets the order of the 90-day roadmap — conversion plumbing before polish.
Booking flow
The self-serve path a visitor takes from "I want this" to a confirmed booking with a real price on screen — no phone call, no waiting for a callback.
The fix for the single biggest leak. The old site ends every path at call us or fill a form and wait 24–48h — confirm; the rebuilt flow (book.html plus the hero quote-card on every page) gets a 2–3 bedroom home to an exact price in about 60 seconds and confirms with a card, $0 charged today. The flow itself carries the trust assets — the trust-bar stays visible the whole way, so trust sits inside the path rather than on a page the buyer never reaches. This is Phase 1, and everything else in the plan supports it.
Trust-bar
A persistent strip directly under the hero that states Marigold's four trust facts on every page, so the nervous buyer sees them without hunting.
Insured & bonded · W-2 team · background-checked · 24-hour re-clean guarantee. These are the exact things a "who am I letting into my house" buyer wants, and on the old site they lived in a footer line nobody read — the most expensive, most defensible asset made the least visible. The trust-bar moves them into the line of sight and, critically, keeps them present through the booking flow, where the decision actually gets made. It is the visible front of Marigold's moat.
Access gate
The buyer's silent first question — "can I even buy this right now, by myself?" — that the site has to clear before anything else matters.
In this category the buyer is often deciding at 11pm after a long day, card in hand. If the only way through is "call us" or "wait for a callback," the access gate is shut and the sale dies at the threshold regardless of how good the cleaning is. The rebuilt booking flow and the on-screen price exist to open this gate. It is the first of two gates every visitor passes through — clear access first, because no amount of credibility helps a buyer who can't act on it.
Credibility gate
The buyer's second silent question — "can I trust whoever's coming into my home?" — that has to clear before they'll hand over a card.
Once the access gate is open and a buyer can book, this is what decides whether they do. It's where the W-2 team, background checks, same-team consistency, and the 24-hour guarantee earn their keep — which is why they belong in the trust-bar and inside the booking flow, not on a separate About page the buyer reaches after they've already decided. Open access, then clear credibility, in that order, and the booking follows.
